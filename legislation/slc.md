# The AI Commons Resolution — Salt Lake City

**A Resolution of the City Council of Salt Lake City, Utah, Establishing the AI Commons Resolution and Urging the Utah State Legislature to Enact Comprehensive Artificial Intelligence Accountability Legislation**

*Status: Draft · 2026*

---

WHEREAS, artificial intelligence systems are rapidly transforming the economy, labor markets, and public life in Utah and across the nation; and

WHEREAS, leading economists and technologists project that AI-driven automation may expose hundreds of millions of jobs globally to significant displacement, with disproportionate impacts on working-class and middle-income Utahns; and

WHEREAS, the productivity gains arising from AI are currently accruing primarily to technology companies and their shareholders, rather than to the workers, communities, and public institutions whose data, labor, and publicly funded research have contributed to these systems; and

WHEREAS, on May 10, 1869, the final spike of the transcontinental railroad was driven at [Promontory Summit, Utah](https://www.nps.gov/gosp/index.htm), connecting the continent and transforming American labor markets in ways that demanded new civic and legislative responses — establishing Utah's place at the intersection of technological transformation and democratic accountability; and

WHEREAS, in 1870, the [Utah Territory granted women the right to vote](https://ilovehistory.utah.gov/1870-1896-womens-suffrage/), making Utah women among the first in the United States to cast ballots — a radical act of democratic inclusion that the U.S. Congress stripped away through the Edmunds-Tucker Act of 1887, and which Utah's founders restored when they enshrined women's suffrage in the state constitution at statehood in 1896, reflecting a tradition of fighting for the democratic participation of all people, even against institutional resistance; and

WHEREAS, Salt Lake City anchors the [Silicon Slopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Slopes) technology corridor, home to more than one thousand tech companies, sixty-seven thousand tech professionals, and over thirty billion dollars in annual economic output — making Utah's capital uniquely positioned both to benefit from AI and to bear the costs of its unaccountable development; and

WHEREAS, Utah's state symbol is the beehive — drawn from the original name "Deseret," meaning honeybee — and its state motto "Industry" reflects a foundational conviction that prosperity is built through collective labor and shared effort, a vision that is fundamentally incompatible with an AI economy that concentrates the gains of automation among a handful of shareholders while displacing the workers whose knowledge and data trained these systems; and

WHEREAS, in 2023, Utah became the first state in the nation to require age verification and parental consent before minors could join social media platforms, through the Utah Social Media Regulation Act (S.B. 152 / H.B. 311) — and in 2024 strengthened those protections with the Minor Protection in Social Media Act (S.B. 194 / H.B. 464), which created a private right of action and minimum damages when platform algorithms cause harm to minors; and

WHEREAS, in 2024, Utah became the [first state in the nation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Utah_Artificial_Intelligence_Policy_Act) to enact legislation specifically regulating generative AI, through the Utah Artificial Intelligence Policy Act (SB 149), establishing disclosure requirements and holding companies responsible for violations; and

WHEREAS, in 2026, the Utah Legislature passed H.B. 286, the Artificial Intelligence Transparency Act, requiring frontier AI companies to publish child safety plans and submit to third-party safety assessments — and when the Trump administration issued a memo calling it "an unfixable bill that goes against the Administration's AI Agenda" and pressed for a veto, the State of Utah held its ground, demonstrating that child protection from AI harm is a legitimate and urgent state interest that cannot be preempted by federal deference to the industry; and

WHEREAS, in December 2025, Governor Spencer Cox launched Utah's "Pro-Human AI" initiative, stating that while he opposes regulation of AI development broadly, "the minute you decide to use those tools to give my kid a sexualized chatbot, then it's my business, and it's the government's business" — reflecting a broad Utah consensus that corporate accountability for AI harms, especially to children, is a legitimate exercise of state power; and

WHEREAS, UC Berkeley Professor Stuart Russell and more than one thousand researchers and scientists signed a statement in 2023 declaring that "[mitigating the risk of extinction from AI should be a global priority alongside other societal-scale risks such as pandemics and nuclear war](https://www.safe.ai/statement-on-ai-risk)" — reflecting a broad scientific consensus that the current trajectory of AI development poses risks not merely to livelihoods but to human civilization itself; and

WHEREAS, New York Assemblyman and Congressional candidate [Alex Bores](https://www.axios.com/2026/04/20/alex-bores-ai-dividend-plan-wealth) — a former AI researcher and co-author of New York's frontier AI safety law — unveiled an AI Dividend plan in April 2026, proposing that if AI dramatically increases productivity and concentrates wealth, the American people have a stake in those gains, to be funded by taxes on AI use and equity stakes in AI companies; and

WHEREAS, OpenAI itself, in its April 2026 economic policy blueprint [*Industrial Policy for the Intelligence Age*](https://openai.com/global-affairs/openais-economic-blueprint/), proposed a national public wealth fund modeled on Alaska's Permanent Fund to distribute AI-driven gains directly to American citizens, and acknowledged that AI-driven automation may shrink payroll tax revenues and threaten Social Security, Medicaid, and other public programs — underscoring the urgency of proactive legislative action; and

WHEREAS, this convergence of proposals across the political and industry spectrum — from progressive legislators to AI companies themselves — reflects a growing consensus that AI's economic gains must be broadly shared, and that unaccountable AI poses risks requiring urgent governmental response; and

WHEREAS, Utah is home to a rapidly expanding AI industry within Silicon Slopes, and the Utah State Legislature has both the authority and the obligation to protect Utahns from the harms of unaccountable AI systems while ensuring the gains of this technology are shared broadly; now, therefore, be it

RESOLVED, That the City Council of Salt Lake City urges the Utah State Legislature to enact policies ensuring that the gains of AI-driven economic growth are broadly shared — particularly with workers and communities experiencing displacement from automation — rather than concentrated among the shareholders of technology companies; and be it further

RESOLVED, That the City Council urges the Utah State Legislature to establish robust accountability standards for AI developers and deployers, ensuring that individuals, families, and communities harmed by AI systems have meaningful and enforceable recourse; and be it further

RESOLVED, That the City Council urges the Utah State Legislature to create strong democratic oversight of AI — including transparency requirements, independent auditing authority, and meaningful civil society participation in governance — so that the technology shaping Utahns' lives is ultimately accountable to them; and be it further

RESOLVED, That the City Clerk is directed to transmit a certified copy of this resolution to the Governor of Utah, the President of the Utah State Senate, the Speaker of the Utah House of Representatives, and the Mayor and City Council of each municipality in Salt Lake County.
