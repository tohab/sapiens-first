Resolution

Berkeley has led on free speech, nuclear disarmament, environmental protection, and civil rights. It's time to lead again.

Why Berkeley

Berkeley has always been where California's future takes shape first. From the Free Speech Movement to landmark environmental protections to sanctuary city status, our city has a tradition of acting on principle before the rest of the state catches up. AI accountability is the next chapter in that tradition.

The University of California, Berkeley, is one of the world's foremost AI research institutions. Our community has seen this technology up close — its promise and its risks — which gives Berkeley both a special credibility and a special responsibility to lead.

Our Ask

This resolution urges the California State Legislature to act on three fronts.

  1. AI Dividend. If AI displaces Californians at scale, the productivity gains should be shared broadly. The Legislature should establish a public dividend tied to AI-driven economic growth — giving working Californians a real stake in the technology reshaping their lives. This proposal has precedent: it has been advanced by policymakers and endorsed by OpenAI's own economic blueprint.
  2. Legal Liability. Right now, developers of frontier AI systems bear almost no legal accountability for harms their systems cause. The Legislature should enact strict liability standards — ensuring that Californians harmed by AI have real, enforceable recourse. Legal scholars have made the case for applying to AI the same accountability standards we hold pharmaceuticals, aviation, and nuclear energy to.
  3. Democratic Oversight. No corporation and no government should control AI alone. The Legislature should create an independent oversight body with authority to audit AI systems, set safety standards, and mandate civil society participation in governance. Researchers have proposed concrete governance frameworks for such a body, including models for meaningful public participation in AI oversight decisions.
The Resolution

The model text below may be adopted by the Berkeley City Council and transmitted to the California State Legislature.

A Resolution of the City Council of Berkeley, California,
Establishing the Berkeley AI Commons Resolution and Urging
the California State Legislature to Enact Comprehensive
Artificial Intelligence Accountability Legislation

WHEREAS, artificial intelligence systems are rapidly transforming the economy, labor markets, and public life in California and across the nation; and

WHEREAS, leading economists and technologists project that AI-driven automation may expose hundreds of millions of jobs globally to significant displacement, with disproportionate impacts on working-class and middle-income Californians; and

WHEREAS, the productivity gains arising from AI are currently accruing primarily to technology companies and their shareholders, rather than to the workers, communities, and public institutions whose data, labor, and publicly funded research have contributed to these systems; and

WHEREAS, in 1964, students at the University of California, Berkeley launched the Free Speech Movement, which transformed the national conversation about civil liberties and demonstrated that principled civic action originating in Berkeley can reshape policy across the country; and

WHEREAS, in 1971, Berkeley became the first city in the United States to declare itself a sanctuary city, through Resolution #44,784, offering refuge to Navy sailors opposed to the Vietnam War — language and philosophy later borrowed by policymakers nationwide to protect refugees and immigrants; and

WHEREAS, in 1986, Berkeley voters passed the Nuclear Free Berkeley Act, establishing the city as a nuclear-free zone and demonstrating that a city can take a principled stand against the power of entrenched industrial and technological interests — and that local action can seed national movements; and

WHEREAS, in 2018, the Berkeley City Council adopted a Climate Emergency Declaration and a Fossil-Free Resolution committing the city to 100% renewable energy by 2030, establishing a precedent of treating technological and industrial transition as a matter of democratic accountability and public welfare; and

WHEREAS, each of these actions reflected Berkeley's conviction that cities bear a responsibility to name emerging threats to human welfare before larger institutions are willing to act — and that Berkeley has done so, repeatedly, to the ultimate benefit of people far beyond its borders; and

WHEREAS, the University of California, Berkeley, is one of the world's foremost AI research institutions, home to the Center for Human-Compatible Artificial Intelligence and the Berkeley Artificial Intelligence Research Lab; and Berkeley is also home to the Machine Intelligence Research Institute, an independent organization dedicated specifically to ensuring that advanced AI does not pose an existential threat to humanity — making this city a global center of gravity for both AI capability and AI safety research; and

WHEREAS, UC Berkeley Professor Stuart Russell and more than one thousand researchers and scientists signed a statement in 2023 declaring that "mitigating the risk of extinction from AI should be a global priority alongside other societal-scale risks such as pandemics and nuclear war" — reflecting a broad scientific consensus that the current trajectory of AI development poses risks not merely to livelihoods but to human civilization itself; and

WHEREAS, New York Assemblyman and Congressional candidate Alex Bores — a former AI researcher and co-author of New York's frontier AI safety law — unveiled an AI Dividend plan in April 2026, proposing that if AI dramatically increases productivity and concentrates wealth, the American people have a stake in those gains, to be funded by taxes on AI use and equity stakes in AI companies; and

WHEREAS, OpenAI itself, in its April 2026 economic policy blueprint Industrial Policy for the Intelligence Age, proposed a national public wealth fund modeled on Alaska's Permanent Fund to distribute AI-driven gains directly to American citizens, and acknowledged that AI-driven automation may shrink payroll tax revenues and threaten Social Security, Medicaid, and other public programs — underscoring the urgency of proactive legislative action; and

WHEREAS, this convergence of proposals across the political and industry spectrum — from progressive legislators to AI companies themselves — reflects a growing consensus that AI's economic gains must be broadly shared, and that unaccountable AI poses risks requiring urgent governmental response; and

WHEREAS, California is home to many of the world's leading AI companies and research institutions, and the California State Legislature has both the authority and the obligation to protect Californians from the harms of unaccountable AI systems while ensuring the gains of this technology are shared broadly; now, therefore, be it

RESOLVED, That the City Council of the City of Berkeley urges the California State Legislature to enact legislation establishing a public AI dividend that distributes a meaningful share of AI-driven productivity gains to all Californians, with priority given to workers and communities experiencing labor market displacement as a result of AI adoption; and be it further

RESOLVED, That the City Council urges the California State Legislature to enact strict liability standards for developers and deployers of frontier AI systems, ensuring that individuals and communities harmed by such systems have meaningful and enforceable legal recourse; and be it further

RESOLVED, That the City Council urges the California State Legislature to establish an independent AI oversight body with authority to audit AI systems, set safety and transparency standards, and ensure meaningful civil society representation in AI governance decisions affecting Californians; and be it further

RESOLVED, That the City Clerk is directed to transmit a certified copy of this resolution to the Governor of California, the President pro Tempore of the California State Senate, the Speaker of the California State Assembly, and the Mayor and City Council of each city in Alameda County.
