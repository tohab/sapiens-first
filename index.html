Public opinion strongly favors AI oversight. But it hasn't translated into political change.

The industry has spent hundreds of millions to keep government away. Ordinary people's concerns — job loss, algorithmic harm, democratic accountability — go unheard.

02 THEORY OF CHANGE

Political movements don't win in Washington first. They win at home — then build.

We start where people live. City by city, we pass resolutions, grow our base, and demonstrate that the public will for AI accountability is real. Then we scale — to statehouses, then Congress.

01
City Council Resolutions
Starting in Berkeley, we pass local resolutions calling for AI accountability. Each resolution is a proof of concept, a coalition-builder, and a media moment that maps public will onto the record.
02
State Laws
We scale from cities to statehouses — organizing constituent pressure in Sacramento and beyond. A 2028 California ballot initiative brings the fight to millions of voters and creates national precedent.
03
Federal Policy
With organized, vocal constituents in every district, we hold Congress accountable. The political cost of inaction becomes higher than the cost of reform.
03 POLICY

We know exactly what we're fighting for. Three policies that would meaningfully constrain AI's worst risks.

01
AI Dividend
The benefits of AI shouldn't accrue only to its developers. We push for a public dividend — a share of AI-generated value returned to the people whose data, labor, and society made it possible.
02
Legal Liability
When AI systems cause harm, developers should be accountable. We fight to close the liability gaps that let AI companies escape responsibility for algorithmic discrimination, job displacement, and safety failures.
03
Independent Oversight
AI is too consequential to self-regulate. We advocate for a dedicated federal agency with real authority over the most powerful technology companies in the world — independent from both industry and short-term political pressure.
